# Singapore Florist Platform Concept

**Stakeholder Summary**  
**Date:** 10 July 2026

## 1. Core Business Idea

Build a marketplace and operations platform for Singapore florists.

The platform helps consumers discover and order from independent florists. It also helps florists manage orders, delivery, pickup, capacity, and customer communication.

Initial comparison: “GrabFood for flowers.”

Refined concept: a curated marketplace for independent florists, not a generic flower catalogue.

## 2. Recommended Starting Niche

Start with flowers, not all home-based businesses.

Focus on:

- Home-based florists
- Independent florist studios
- Small commercial florists
- Preserved and dried flower sellers
- Event florists who also want ad hoc gifting orders

Do not start by depending on large incumbents such as FarEastFlora, Express Flower, or Flower Chimp. Large florists already have SEO, logistics, inventory, advertising, and brand awareness. The sharper opportunity is helping smaller florists compete.

## 3. Why Florist-First Makes Sense

Florists face a specific set of problems:

- Poor SEO visibility
- Uneven exposure versus large online florists
- Instagram and WhatsApp-heavy manual ordering
- Delivery coordination pain
- Fragile products
- Seasonal spikes during Valentine’s Day, Mother’s Day, graduations, and other occasions
- Limited daily production capacity

Consumers face pain too:

- It is hard to compare florist styles.
- It is hard to know who is available for a specific date.
- Delivery fees are unclear.
- Buying flowers is emotionally high-stakes.
- Late, damaged, or incorrect deliveries create anxiety.

## 4. TAM Concern

Florist-only may be too small if the business remains limited to Singapore flowers only. Public Singapore flower-market estimates vary heavily, so exact TAM numbers should be treated carefully.

The florist niche may be strong enough for an MVP, but may not be large enough for a large-scale company without expansion. Starting too broadly with all home-based businesses is also risky. A broad home-based product marketplace would compete with Shopee, Carousell, TikTok Shop, and Instagram.

## 5. Recommended Expansion Strategy

Start florist-first. Expand later into **local occasion gifting**, not all small businesses.

Natural adjacent categories include:

- Preserved flowers
- Cakes
- Gift boxes
- Handmade cards
- Candles
- Small jewellery
- Proposal, graduation, and birthday gifts
- Corporate gifts

This keeps the platform focused around one buyer mission: **“I need a thoughtful local gift delivered or ready for pickup.”**

### Discussion outcome — Marcus and Justin

Marcus raised the concern that expansion could make the platform too complicated. Justin noted that the Singapore florist market is small and highly specific, and considered whether the concept could eventually serve home-based businesses selling other products.

The agreed direction remains to begin with a focused florist niche. The possible expansion into other home-based businesses should be kept in mind, but not incorporated into the initial launch. Expansion should first follow the coherent local occasion-gifting categories above rather than becoming a generic home-based business marketplace.

## 6. Proposed USP

### For sellers

Massively increase their outreach and make them more visible to buyers who would otherwise be unaware of them.

### For consumers

- A curated one-stop website or app for local businesses
- Search by design preference and location, with a proposed built-in map to optimise purchases
- One-stop comparison between businesses by location, price, design, and delivery availability

Long-form positioning:

> A curated marketplace for independent Singapore florists and occasion gifts, with real availability, self-pickup, scheduled delivery, and seller contact built in.

Shorter positioning options:

- “Beautiful flowers from local florists, delivered without the chasing.”
- “Discover independent Singapore florists, with pickup and delivery handled.”
- “Local flowers and thoughtful gifts, ordered without Instagram DMs.”

## 7. Consumer Flow

### 1. Search

The consumer searches according to their preferences:

- Occasion
- Delivery date
- Budget
- Style
- Flower type
- Delivery postal code
- Pickup location
- Delivery window

### 2. Discover

The platform displays only products and florists that are available for the consumer’s selected date and fulfilment method.

### 3. Choose

The consumer selects an available fulfilment mode:

- Self-pickup
- Standard scheduled delivery
- Priority or express delivery

Delivery options should appear only when they are supported by the seller or by a validated platform delivery arrangement. For the initial pickup-first launch, self-pickup will be the primary option.

### 4. Personalise

The consumer adds the relevant delivery or pickup details:

- Recipient name
- Recipient phone number
- Delivery address, where applicable
- Card message
- Pickup or delivery instructions

### 5. Connect

The consumer can message the seller for:

- Customisation
- Substitution approval
- Pickup coordination
- Order questions or confirmation

### 6. Place Order

The consumer completes the secure ordering sequence:

**Review order → Payment → Confirmation**

## 8. Seller Flow

### 1. Create Profile

The seller builds their florist profile and offerings by adding:

- Brand story
- Photos
- Product listings
- Style tags
- Pickup location
- Operating days
- Capacity limits
- Lead times

### 2. Manage Operations

The seller sets up and manages daily operations through:

- Incoming orders
- Daily capacity
- Pickup windows
- Delivery readiness
- Customer messages
- Payouts

### 3. Manage Orders

The seller tracks each order and updates its status in real time:

**Accepted → Preparing → Ready for pickup → Fulfilled**

Where delivery is used, “ready for pickup” refers to the order being ready for collection by either the customer or the seller’s delivery provider.

### 4. Deliver and Complete

Once fulfilment is complete:

- The order is marked complete.
- The customer receives a notification.
- The seller’s payout is processed.
- The completed order contributes to the seller’s customer relationships and business growth.

The intended outcome is: **happy customers, smooth operations, and sustainable growth.**

## 9. Delivery and Pickup Model

Self-pickup should be a core option, not an afterthought. It helps lower costs and suits home-based florists. For home-based sellers, exact addresses can be revealed only after payment for privacy.

### Initial-launch approach

The initial launch should be **pickup-first** because the platform will not yet have enough order volume to operate an economical delivery network or reliably batch orders.

Where buyers require delivery during the initial phase, delivery should go through the seller’s existing arrangement while the platform researches and validates suitable courier options for fragile products. The platform should not build or operate its own delivery fleet at this stage.

If the platform later achieves sufficient order density, it can work with third-party delivery companies and coordinate or batch orders. This follows the marketplace model of partnering with logistics providers rather than maintaining an internal fleet.

### Future platform-coordinated delivery model

If courier research and actual order volume support platform-coordinated delivery:

- Standard delivery should use time windows rather than instant delivery.
- Example windows could be 10am–2pm, 2pm–6pm, and 6pm–9pm.
- Priority delivery could be available at a premium.
- Exact-time delivery should not be the default because it is expensive.

These delivery windows and service tiers are proposals to validate, not confirmed launch commitments.

## 10. Delivery Cost Logic

A default delivery fee around SGD 20 is likely too high. SGD 20 or more may be acceptable only for urgent express or fragile exact-time delivery.

The previously proposed tiers are:

- Self-pickup: free
- Standard scheduled delivery: target SGD 8–12
- Priority or express delivery: SGD 18–30+
- Remote or surcharge zones: extra fee

Lower delivery costs would likely require:

- Wider time windows
- Batching by postal district
- Grouped florist pickups
- Advance ordering
- Multi-stop courier routes
- Zone-based pricing
- A self-pickup option

### Discussion outcome — Marcus and Justin

Marcus identified two risks with the proposed delivery prices:

1. The platform might lose money when order scale is still low.
2. A high delivery charge could repel more customers than expected.

Justin noted that delivery coordination is still an important adoption reason for the platform; without addressing it in some form, the platform’s consumer value may be limited mainly to reach and marketing.

The resulting position is to avoid promising or subsidising low platform-arranged delivery prices before the economics are validated. Courier pricing for fragile products must be researched first. The concierge MVP should record real seller and courier charges before delivery tiers are finalised.

Some florists reportedly display a low delivery fee, such as SGD 5, while absorbing the remaining delivery cost into the flower price. This pricing practice should be investigated during seller interviews rather than assumed as the platform’s model. Larger florists such as Xpressflower and FarEastFlora may have their own delivery arrangements, while smaller sellers may not have equivalent scale.

## 11. Delivery Partner Thinking

All partner assessments below are hypotheses that require validation, especially for fragile flower arrangements:

- **GrabExpress:** potentially suitable for urgent fragile delivery, but likely too expensive as the default
- **Lalamove:** potentially useful for same-day delivery, vehicle choice, scheduled delivery, and multi-stop routes; batching depends on sufficient order density
- **uParcel:** worth testing for same-day and fragile flower or cake-style deliveries. Strong selection thus far for 6 hour delievery window scheduling svc and low prices.
- **Ninja Van:** potentially suitable for non-fragile parcels, next-day delivery, and standard e-commerce logistics; less naturally suited to same-day fresh flowers, but strong because has Ninja Express and other stuff
- **J&T Express:** potentially suitable for standard non-fragile products, especially if the platform later expands into packaged home-based goods

### Research required before selecting partners

- How couriers define and accept fragile items
- Whether fresh flowers are covered, restricted, or excluded
- Damage, delay, and compensation policies
- Vehicle types and handling requirements
- Point-to-point versus multi-stop pricing
- Scheduled-window and exact-time pricing
- Minimum volume requirements and business rates
- Who is liable when an arrangement is damaged

## 12. Independent Seller Delivery Research

Non-fragile home-based sellers often use cheap postage or standard parcel delivery. Example categories include press-on nails, accessories, cards, and small gifts.

Fragile or custom sellers often prefer self-collection or same-day courier. Example categories include flowers, cakes, and delicate 3D-printed objects.

Established florists often advertise “free delivery,” but delivery may be embedded in the product price. Home-based sellers often quote delivery manually through direct messages or WhatsApp. Both observations should be validated through seller interviews.

## 13. Business Model Options

### Commission per order

Recommended MVP model. Possible range: 12–15%.

### Delivery fee

Charged to the consumer. The platform may later earn a margin if volume rates improve, but should not depend on this before courier costs are validated.

### Seller subscription

Possible future Pro tier, which could include lower commission, storefront tools, analytics, calendar features, and priority exposure.

### Seasonal packages

Packages for Valentine’s Day, Mother’s Day, graduation season, and corporate gifting could include delivery coordination and peak-order management.

## 14. MVP Feature Set

### Consumer marketplace

- Browse florists
- Search by date, budget, occasion, and location
- Product pages
- Self-pickup option
- Seller-managed delivery information or option, where available
- Checkout
- Order tracking
- Seller contact or chat

Platform-coordinated scheduled and priority delivery should be added only after courier feasibility and pricing are validated.

### Seller dashboard

- Profile
- Product listings
- Capacity calendar
- Order inbox
- Pickup windows
- Ready-for-pickup button
- Customer messaging
- Payout overview

### Admin dashboard

- Approve sellers
- Moderate listings
- Monitor orders
- Handle fulfilment exceptions
- Manage peak-date capacity
- Support manual delivery coordination during tests, if offered

## 15. What Not to Build First

- Do not build instant delivery as the default.
- Do not build a full route-optimisation engine immediately.
- Do not launch with every home-based business category.
- Do not build native mobile apps first.
- Do not allow unlimited open seller signup at the beginning.
- Do not depend on delivery margin before courier costs are validated.
- Do not build an internal delivery fleet for the initial launch.
- Do not hide the florist’s identity; the florist’s brand is part of the product.

## 16. Main Risks

- Singapore florist-only TAM may be limited.
- Marketplace chicken-and-egg problem
- Sellers may bypass the platform after meeting customers.
- Seller contact features can increase bypass risk.
- Delivery failures can damage trust quickly.
- Peak seasons create demand but also the highest operational risk.
- Large florist incumbents may copy visible marketplace features.
- “Free delivery” consumer expectations may pressure margins.
- Delivery prices may cause either platform losses or customer drop-off before sufficient scale is reached.

## 17. Main Strengths

- Clear niche with real pain
- Independent florists are underserved by SEO-heavy discovery.
- Flowers are high-intent, emotional, occasion-based purchases.
- Delivery and pickup coordination are real operational problems.
- Self-pickup can reduce delivery cost and increase seller adoption.
- The platform can expand naturally into occasion gifting.
- Seller capacity and fulfilment data can become a defensible advantage.

## 18. Recommended Validation Plan

Interview 20–30 florists, including:

- Home-based florists
- Small studios
- Preserved flower sellers
- Event florists

Ask sellers:

- How they currently get orders
- How they handle delivery
- Which couriers they use for fragile arrangements
- Whether delivery is charged separately or embedded in product prices
- How much they pay for couriers
- Whether they allow self-pickup
- Their daily capacity
- Their peak-season pain points
- Whether they would pay commission for incremental orders

Mystery-shop 50 sellers across flowers and adjacent gift categories.

Run a concierge MVP before building full software:

- Onboard 10–15 florists.
- Use a simple landing page.
- Make pickup the primary fulfilment option.
- Allow sellers to use their existing delivery arrangements where needed.
- Manually test suitable fragile-item couriers on a limited basis.
- Track actual delivery costs, damage rates, delivery failures, and buyer conversion.

## 19. MVP Success Criteria

- 10+ florists onboarded
- 100+ orders in 60–90 days or clear seasonal traction
- Delivery failure below 3–5% for any delivery tests
- Consumers use discovery pages, not only direct florist links.
- Sellers accept commission because orders are incremental.
- Self-pickup is used enough to reduce delivery pressure.
- Buyers show repeat or reminder-driven behaviour for occasions.
- Courier research establishes whether standard delivery near SGD 8–12 is viable; this is a validation target rather than an assumed outcome.

## 20. Strategic Recommendation

Start with flowers and position the platform around independent Singapore florists. Keep the initial launch pickup-first, with sellers handling delivery through their existing arrangements where necessary.

Research fragile-item courier services and collect real delivery-cost data during the concierge MVP. If order density later becomes sufficient, partner with third-party delivery companies and test scheduled windows, batching, and premium priority delivery. Do not create an internal delivery fleet at the start.

Keep the long-term expansion path focused on local occasion gifting. Retain the broader home-based business opportunity as a future consideration, but avoid making the initial marketplace generic or operationally complex.
